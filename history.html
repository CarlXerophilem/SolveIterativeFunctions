A History of Iterative Functional Equations

Two centuries of one idea — the square root of a function under composition — from Babbage's Calculus of Functions to resurgence theory and the complex-dynamics boom.

This page tells the story behind the mathematics laid out in the Theory chapter and catalogued in the Atlas of Equations. The thread running through it is a single deceptively simple question: given a map \(f\), what does it mean to apply it “half a time”? Formally, an iterative functional equation is an equation whose unknown is a function composed with itself or with a known map — the archetype being the functional (compositional) square root \(\varphi(\varphi(x)) = f(x)\). Over two hundred years this question braided together with the theory of iteration of functions — today a cornerstone of holomorphic and complex dynamics — and produced a small family of canonical equations bearing the names of Babbage, Abel, Schröder, Koenigs and Böttcher. The pages of the Atlas formalise each one; here we watch them being discovered.

How to read the dates. Wherever a date, venue, or attribution is contested in the historical record, this page flags it (“commonly dated to”, “often attributed to”) rather than asserting a single value. Such hedges are deliberate, not vague. The riskiest specifics are gathered for checking in the Bibliography.

1. The Founding Period: Babbage (1815)

The systematic study of functional equations in a single variable begins with Charles Babbage (1791–1871), better remembered today for his calculating engines. His memoir “An Essay towards the Calculus of Functions” was read to the Royal Society on 15 June 1815 (communicated by W. H. Wollaston) and published in the Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society of London, vol. 105 (1815), pp. 389–423; a second part followed in 1816. Babbage, then twenty-three, treated equations containing only one variable far more systematically than any predecessor — the true start of the subject this whole site is built on.

Babbage's equation. Babbage studied the involutions \[ \varphi(\varphi(x)) = x, \] whose solutions are the “roots of identity” — functions equal to their own inverse. More generally he considered the \(n\)-th order equation \(\varphi^{[n]}(x) = x\) (the iterate of \(\varphi\) composed \(n\) times equalling the identity), whose solutions are the iterative roots of the identity, i.e. periodic functions under composition.

The leap from \(\varphi^{[2]} = \mathrm{id}\) to the general target is small but decisive: replacing the right-hand side by an arbitrary \(f\) gives \[ \varphi(\varphi(x)) = f(x), \] which is exactly the functional square root that drives the Solver and the Theory page. Babbage had already isolated two structural facts that the modern theory never outgrew. First, a particular involution: the Möbius family \(\varphi(x) = (b - x)/(1 + cx)\) (for \(bc \neq -1\)) solves \(\varphi(\varphi(x)) = x\). Second — and this is the seed of the entire conjugacy viewpoint — he noticed that if \(\varphi\) is a solution, then so is any conjugate \(\psi^{-1} \circ \varphi \circ \psi\). In modern language, the group of invertible functions acts on the solution set by conjugation.

Babbage's insight that conjugation generates new solutions from old is the historical root of the method the Theory page calls linearization: relabel coordinates until the equation becomes trivial, solve it there, and conjugate back. Everything from Schröder to Böttcher is a way of choosing that coordinate well.
A note on Babbage's original notation

The crisp formula \(\varphi(\varphi(x)) = x\) is the way secondary sources render Babbage's problem; his own 1815 notation differed, and the “\(n\)-th order” framing \(\varphi^{[n]} = \mathrm{id}\) is drawn from later summaries rather than verified line-by-line against the original text. The compositional power notation \(f^{n}\) itself is usually traced to John Herschel (1813), who credited earlier work of Hans Heinrich Bürmann; the bracketed variant \(f^{[n]}\) is associated with Benjamin Peirce. None of this changes the mathematics, but it is a reminder that the “classical” equations on this page were often named and normalised decades after the fact.

2. Abel & the Additive Normal Form

Niels Henrik Abel (1802–1829) supplied the additive companion to Babbage's multiplicative world. The Abel functional equation, \[ \alpha\bigl(f(x)\bigr) = \alpha(x) + 1, \] conjugates the iteration of \(f\) to a unit translation. If \(\alpha\) solves it, then the \(t\)-th iterate is simply \[ f^{[t]}(x) = \alpha^{-1}\bigl(\alpha(x) + t\bigr), \] which interpolates the integer index \(t\) to any real (or complex) value — the precise mechanism by which a half-iterate \(f^{[1/2]} = \alpha^{-1}(\alpha + \tfrac12)\) is obtained on the Theory page.

The dating is genuinely delicate, and worth stating carefully. The Abel equation is commonly attributed to the 1820s, but the directly relevant manuscript — “Détermination d'une fonction au moyen d'une équation qui ne contient qu'une seule variable” — appeared only posthumously, in Abel's Œuvres complètes (edited by Ludwig Sylow and Sophus Lie, Christiania/Oslo, 1881), more than half a century after it was written. An exact composition year for the equation in the form \(\alpha(f) = \alpha + 1\) is not firmly pinned in the sources surveyed here, so it is best to say the equation is of the 1820s, published 1881.

Schröder and Abel are two faces of one normal form. Abel's additive equation is the logarithmic form of Schröder's multiplicative equation. Where Schröder asks for \(\Psi(f(x)) = s\,\Psi(x)\), the substitution \(\alpha = \log\Psi / \log s\) turns multiplication by \(s\) into addition of \(1\). The Abel form is the natural normal form at a parabolic fixed point (multiplier \(s = 1\)), where the multiplicative version degenerates — see §4 and the Theory page.

This additive picture is also the bridge to the modern flow viewpoint: writing the iteration velocity as a vector field \(v(x)\), the continuous index \(t\) realises \(f^{[t]} = \exp(t\,v\,\partial_x)\), and \(v\) is recovered from \(f\) via exactly this Abel equation. The Connections page follows that thread into the embedding-into-a-flow problem and the Aczél–Jabotinsky generator.

3. Schröder & Koenigs: Linearization

The decisive modern reframing came from Ernst Schröder (1841–1902), who published “Ueber iterirte Functionen” in Mathematische Annalen 3 (1870), pp. 296–322. Schröder proposed studying the iteration of a (meromorphic) function \(f\) through what we now call the Schröder equation:

Schröder's equation. \[ \Psi\bigl(f(x)\bigr) = s\,\Psi(x), \qquad \Psi(0) = 0,\ \Psi'(0) \neq 0, \] where the constant \(s\) is the multiplier \(s = f'(0)\). This linearizes the dynamics: the \(n\)-th iterate becomes \(\Psi(f^{[n]}(x)) = s^{n}\Psi(x)\), so in the coordinate \(u = \Psi(x)\) the map \(f\) is the pure dilation \(u \mapsto s\,u\).

Schröder gave striking worked examples in 1870 — including the chaotic logistic case \(f(x) = 4x(1-x)\), for which \(\Psi(x) = \arcsin(\sqrt{x})^{2}\) yields the closed-form iterate \(f^{[n]}(x) = \sin\!\bigl(2^{n}\arcsin\sqrt{x}\bigr)^{2}\). (A historical footnote worth a soft touch: the paper itself is dated 1870, and some later sources phrase the functional-equation proposal as “1871”. Both years circulate; the Annalen volume is 1870.)

Schröder stated the program; Gabriel Koenigs (1858–1931) made it a theorem. In 1884 Koenigs gave the first rigorous analytic theory of the attracting (non-superattracting) case.

Koenigs' theorem (1884). If \(f\) is holomorphic near an attracting fixed point (say \(0\)) with multiplier \(s = f'(0)\) satisfying \(0 < |s| < 1\), then there is a unique holomorphic Koenigs function \(\Psi\), normalised by \(\Psi(0) = 0\) and \(\Psi'(0) = 1\), solving Schröder's equation. Thus \(f\) is locally analytically conjugate to multiplication by \(s\). Koenigs constructed \(\Psi\) explicitly as the limit \[ \Psi = \lim_{n \to \infty} s^{-n}\, f^{[n]}. \]

The repelling case \(|s| > 1\) reduces to the attracting one by passing to \(f^{-1}\); Poincaré linearizers (often called Poincaré functions) extend such local conjugacies to entire functions, a refinement built on Henri Poincaré's linearization ideas. Together, Schröder and Koenigs settle the entire hyperbolic regime \(0 < |s| < 1\) or \(|s| > 1\). The Theory page turns Koenigs' limit into the Solver's local-analytic mode, which expands \(\Psi\) as a power series using the composita recurrences and reads off the half-iterate \(\Psi^{-1}(\sqrt{s}\,\Psi)\).

Koenigs' result is the reason a hyperbolic fixed point with multiplier \(s > 0\) always admits a clean local half-iterate: halving the index just means replacing \(s\) by \(\sqrt{s}\). The whole difficulty of the subject lives in the cases Koenigs excluded — \(s = 0\) (superattracting) and \(|s| = 1\) (parabolic and elliptic).

4. The Parabolic Case & the Leau–Fatou Flower

When the multiplier is a root of unity — the parabolic or rationally indifferent case — simple linearization fails: there is no analytic Schröder function, because the resonance \(s^{n} = s\) blocks the coefficient recurrence (see the Theory sketch). The structure that replaces it was uncovered around the turn of the twentieth century.

Léopold Leau treated the case in his 1897 thesis “Études sur les équations fonctionnelles à une ou à plusieurs variables”, studying iteration near a rationally indifferent fixed point and obtaining the structure now called the (Leau–)Fatou flower theorem. Related algorithmic work came from A. Grévy and E.-M. Lémeray — the latter's “Sur quelques algorithmes généraux et sur l'itération” (1898). Here the local normal form is precisely Abel's equation \(\alpha(f) = \alpha + 1\), whose analytic solutions were later understood as Fatou coordinates.

Leau–Fatou flower theorem (parabolic petals). At a parabolic fixed point of a map \(f(z) = z + a z^{n+1} + \cdots\) with \(a \neq 0\), the local dynamics organise into \(n\) attracting and \(n\) repelling petals, alternating around the fixed point, whose union together with the fixed point forms a punctured neighbourhood (a “flower”). On each petal a conformal Fatou coordinate \(\alpha\) satisfies \(\alpha(f(z)) = \alpha(z) + 1\).

Precise attributions in this corner are mixed in the literature, so a light touch is in order: Leau's 1897 thesis and Lémeray's 1898 title are well attested, while Grévy's exact contribution (a paper on Abel's equation in the real case, of the mid-to-late 1890s) is less firmly pinned in the sources surveyed here. The flower theorem itself is usually credited jointly to Leau and to Fatou, who recast it inside the global theory.

The parabolic case is not a curiosity. It is the local model at multiplier \(s = 1\) — the regime that governs slow, algebraic convergence and the half-iterate of maps like \(x \mapsto x + 1\). It also reappears, much later, as the home of the Écalle–Voronin moduli that completely classify parabolic germs.

5. Böttcher & Superattracting Fixed Points

The last of the four local regimes — the superattracting case, where the multiplier vanishes — was treated by Lucjan Emil Böttcher (1872–1937), described as a Polish pioneer of holomorphic dynamics. When \(f(z) = a z^{k} + O(z^{k+1})\) with \(a \neq 0\) and \(k \ge 2\), the fixed point at the origin attracts quadratically (or faster), and the right model is not a dilation but a power map.

Böttcher's equation. \[ F\bigl(h(z)\bigr) = F(z)^{\,k}, \] linearizes the iteration of a superattracting germ \(h(z) = a z^{k} + \cdots\) to the power map \(z \mapsto z^{k}\). Böttcher's theorem (1904): such a germ is analytically conjugate to \(z \mapsto z^{k}\) by a holomorphic Böttcher coordinate tangent to the identity.

Böttcher sketched the proof in 1904. His original paper appeared in the Russian/Polish-area journal literature of the time, and its exact title and venue are not captured in the sources surveyed here — one reason the result was slow to enter the Western canon. A complete proof was given independently by Joseph Fels Ritt, “On the Iteration of Rational Functions”, Transactions of the American Mathematical Society 21, no. 3 (July 1920), pp. 348–356 — Ritt being unaware of Böttcher's original work. (Sources sometimes write the equation with a general exponent \(n\); for a degree-\(k\) superattracting germ the exponent is the local degree \(k\).)

With Böttcher, all four local fixed-point regimes have a governing equation: Schröder/Koenigs (hyperbolic), Böttcher (superattracting), Abel on petals (parabolic), and the small-divisor Siegel problem (elliptic, discussed on the Connections page). The Theory page's half-iterate table is exactly this four-way split, one normal form per row.

The fixed-point trichotomy, by governing equation

\(0 < |s| < 1,\ |s| > 1\) · hyperbolic Schröder / Koenigs \(u \mapsto s\,u\)
\(s = 0\) · superattracting Böttcher \(u \mapsto u^{k}\)
\(s\) root of unity · parabolic Abel (on petals) \(u \mapsto u + 1\)

The four classical equations are not rivals but a partition of the fixed-point world by multiplier. Full definitions live in the Atlas of Equations.

6. Fatou, Julia & Lattès: the Global Theory

The four local pictures — attracting, repelling, parabolic, superattracting — became the foundation of a global theory of iteration of rational maps of the Riemann sphere, created almost simultaneously and largely independently by Pierre Fatou (1878–1929) and Gaston Julia (1893–1978) around 1918–1920.

The catalyst was a prize. In 1915 the Académie des Sciences announced a Grand Prix des Sciences Mathématiques, to be awarded in 1918, for the iteration of rational substitutions. Julia won it; his “Mémoire sur l'itération des fonctions rationnelles” (some 199 pages) appeared in the Journal de Mathématiques Pures et Appliquées in 1918, written while he was hospitalised after wounds suffered in the First World War. Fatou, working at the same time, published results of comparable depth and incisiveness.

The Fatou–Julia dichotomy. For a rational map of the sphere, the sphere splits into the Fatou set — where the family of iterates is normal (locally equicontinuous), the locus of stable, predictable behaviour — and its complement, the Julia set, the chaotic locus on which the dynamics are sensitive and self-similar. Every local theory of §3–§5 describes the behaviour of orbits as they approach this global skeleton.

Months before his death, Samuel Lattès (1873–1918) published “Sur l'itération des substitutions rationnelles et les fonctions de Poincaré”, Comptes Rendus de l'Académie des Sciences 166 (1918), pp. 26–28, exhibiting the maps now called Lattès maps: rational maps \(f = \Theta \circ L \circ \Theta^{-1}\) obtained by semiconjugating a torus (or affine) endomorphism \(L\) to the sphere via a Weierstrass-type \(\Theta\). Their defining feature is extreme: the Julia set is the entire sphere. Fatou later (from 1926) opened the parallel theory of iteration of transcendental entire functions, a thread picked up at mid-century by I. N. Baker.

This is the moment the “iterative functional equations” of Babbage and the “iteration of functions” of the dynamicists fuse into one subject. The same Schröder, Abel and Böttcher coordinates that solve functional equations now chart the basins and the boundary of the Julia set. The Connections page maps that fusion in detail.

7. Mid-Century Real-Analytic Iteration

The complex theory matured; the real-analytic problem — can one iterate a growing real function a fractional number of times, smoothly, on the whole line? — remained stubborn. Its most famous instance is the half-exponential.

Kneser (1950). Hellmuth Kneser (1898–1973) constructed a real-analytic solution \(\varphi\) of \[ \varphi(\varphi(x)) = e^{x}, \] a genuine “half-iterate of the exponential”, in “Reelle analytische Lösungen der Gleichung \(\varphi(\varphi(x)) = e^{x}\) und verwandter Funktionalgleichungen”, Journal für die reine und angewandte Mathematik (Crelle's Journal) 187 (1950), pp. 56–67. The construction is complex-analytic at heart: an Abel function built at a fixed point, with \(\varphi = g^{-1}(g(x) + \tfrac12)\).

Kneser's method showed that the Abel equation admits infinitely many real-analytic solutions, so the existence of a half-exponential was never the hard part; the subtlety is singling out a canonical one. (The precise statement and scope of any uniqueness theorem for the real-analytic half-iterate of \(\exp\) on all of \(\mathbb{R}\) varies by author, and the year “1950” here follows the standard secondary attributions of Kneser's Crelle paper.) This question feeds directly into the modern tetration literature, surveyed on the Connections page.

George Szekeres (1911–2005) systematised the general apparatus of regular (fractional) iteration in “Regular iteration of real and complex functions”, Acta Mathematica 100 (1958), pp. 203–258, and later studied the fractional iteration of exponentially growing functions. A complementary, algebraic line is the Aczél–Jabotinsky approach to continuous iteration via infinite matrices and an infinitesimal-generator differential equation.

The Jabotinsky matrix line. Eri Jabotinsky represented functional composition by infinite matrices (a note in C. R. Acad. Sci. Paris 224 (1947), pp. 323–324) and developed continuous iteration through the Aczél–Jabotinsky differential equation in “Analytic Iteration”, Transactions of the AMS 108 (1963), pp. 457–477 — the joint name recognising related work of János Aczél. The same matrix idea, in coefficient form, is what the Composita Calculator implements as a recurrence.

8. The Silesian School & Consolidation

By mid-century the field had a wealth of results but no unifying treatise. The decisive consolidation was the work of Marek Kuczma (1935–1991) at the University of Silesia in Katowice, who is widely regarded as the creator of the modern systematic theory of iterative functional equations and the founder of the Polish (Silesian) school of functional equations.

  • M. Kuczma, Functional Equations in a Single Variable, PWN, Warsaw, 1968 (Monografie Matematyczne 46) — the foundational monograph. 1968
  • M. Kuczma, B. Choczewski & R. Ger, Iterative Functional Equations, Encyclopedia of Mathematics and its Applications 32, Cambridge University Press, 1990 (576 pp.) — the definitive reference work. 1990
  • J. Aczél, Lectures on Functional Equations and Their Applications, Academic Press, 1966 (German original Birkhäuser, 1961) — the standard text for the broader community. 1966
  • Gy. Targonski, Topics in Iteration Theory, Vandenhoeck & Ruprecht, Göttingen, 1981 — a field-shaping survey. 1981

Around Kuczma's books grew the institutions of the field. János Aczél (1924–2020) anchored the wider functional-equations community and founded its flagship journal, Aequationes Mathematicae, where the iteration-theory surveys and conference proceedings still appear. György Targonski was a central organiser and editor of the field's meetings — he ran the 1980 Marburg conference and wrote the 1981 survey above — though it is more accurate to call him a leading organiser than the sole “founder” of the conference series, which predates his book.

The ECIT series. The European Conference on Iteration Theory (ECIT) began in Toulouse in 1973, continued in Graz (1977) and Marburg (1980), and runs to this day (recent editions include 2014 and 2022). Its proceedings, alongside Aequationes Mathematicae, are the field's institutional memory.

On the dynamics side, I. N. Baker (1932–2001) founded the modern theory of iteration of transcendental entire functions, extending Fatou's 1926 program. His most celebrated discovery is that an entire function can possess a wandering domain — a component of the Fatou set never mapped back to itself or to a periodic one (An entire function which has wandering domains, J. Austral. Math. Soc., 1976). This is impossible for rational maps, by Dennis Sullivan's later no-wandering-domains theorem — a striking dividing line between the rational and entire worlds.

9. The Modern Revival

The closing chapter is a revival on three fronts: a deep analytic completion of the parabolic case, the Mandelbrot-era explosion of complex dynamics, and a surprising re-emergence in physics.

Jean Écalle developed resurgence theory and mould calculus (Les fonctions résurgentes, Publications Mathématiques d'Orsay 81-05 and 81-06, 1981), giving a complete account of the generically divergent Fatou coordinates of §4 and reconstructing the Écalle–Voronin moduli — the invariants that completely classify parabolic germs up to analytic conjugacy. This is the long-delayed resolution of the one local case that resisted Schröder, Koenigs and Böttcher alike.

Meanwhile the Mandelbrot-era boom — Douady, Hubbard, Sullivan, Milnor and many others — revived complex dynamics as a major field, with Böttcher and Koenigs coordinates as everyday tools (Böttcher coordinates, for instance, define the external rays that organise the Mandelbrot set). The local equations of the nineteenth century became the working vocabulary of late-twentieth-century research.

Iteration in physics. Thomas Curtright and Cosmas Zachos recast time evolution and renormalization-group flow in the language of Schröder's equation (“Evolution profiles and functional equations”, J. Phys. A, 2009, arXiv:0909.2424; “Renormalization Group Functional Equations”, 2010, arXiv:1010.5174), building exact continuous iterations (e.g. of \(2x(1+x)\)) and approximate continuous iterates of \(x\,e^{x}\). An RG flow is literally a continuous iteration; Schröder's 1870 equation is its natural normal form.
Two centuries on, the arc closes on itself. Babbage asked for the square root of a function; the answer turned out to require a coordinate in which composition becomes arithmetic — multiplication (Schröder), addition (Abel), or raising to a power (Böttcher). The Solver on this site is a direct descendant: it computes those very coordinates, by series and by linearization, to produce half-iterates on demand. Continue to the Theory page to see the machinery at work.

  • 1870 Schröder — Ueber iterirte Functionen; the Schröder equation \(\Psi(f(x)) = s\,\Psi(x)\).
