Atlas of the Named Equations

A field guide to the functional equations that govern iteration — one clearly-headed entry per equation, organised by the geometry of fixed points. The companion to the Theory page, which derives the central three.

1. How to Read This Atlas: the Multiplier Map

Nearly every equation on this page is a way of saying the same thing twice: relabel the coordinate so that a complicated map becomes a simple one we can iterate by inspection. The Theory page develops this idea — conjugacy — in detail. Here we catalogue the equations themselves, because the same handful of normal forms recurs across a century and a half of work under different names.

The organising invariant is the multiplier of a fixed point. If a map \(F\) fixes a point \(x^*\) (so \(F(x^*)=x^*\)) and is differentiable there, its multiplier is the derivative \[ \lambda = F'(x^*). \] Under an analytic (or smooth) change of coordinate the multiplier is unchanged: it is a conjugacy invariant (see Milnor, Dynamics in One Complex Variable). So the value of \(\lambda\) sorts the local linearization problem into a small number of regimes, and each regime is governed by its own canonical equation.

The four regimes, and who governs each.
  • Hyperbolic — \(0<|\lambda|<1\) (attracting) or \(|\lambda|>1\) (repelling): Schröder / Koenigs linearization to \(u\mapsto \lambda u\).
  • Superattracting — \(\lambda=0\), with local degree \(n\ge2\): Böttcher conjugacy to the power map \(w\mapsto w^{\,n}\).
  • Parabolic / rationally indifferent — \(\lambda\) a root of unity (canonically \(\lambda=1\)): Abel's equation / Fatou coordinates / the Leau–Fatou flower.
  • Irrationally indifferent — \(|\lambda|=1\) with \(\lambda\) not a root of unity: the Siegel (linearizable) versus Cremer (non-linearizable) dichotomy, with small-divisor difficulties that sit outside the classical Schröder/Abel/Böttcher trio.

Read this way, Schröder, Böttcher and Abel are not three unrelated tricks but a partition of one problem — the linearization of a germ — according to where \(\lambda\) lands. The remaining entries either generalise this (iterative roots, conjugacy), differentiate it (Julia, Aczél–Jabotinsky), or globalise it (translation, commuting maps). The field-map below shows the three classical regimes side by side; the irrational case is flagged as the hard frontier.

2. Babbage's Equation: \(F^{\circ n}=\mathrm{id}\)

The starting point of the whole subject asks for the maps that undo themselves after a fixed number of steps.

Babbage's equation. \[ F^{\circ n}(x)=x,\qquad F^{\circ n}=\underbrace{F\circ\cdots\circ F}_{n}. \] Its solutions are the periodic (cyclic) functions of period dividing \(n\), i.e. the \(n\)-th iterative roots of the identity. For \(n=2\) they are the involutions, \(F\circ F=\mathrm{id}\).

Named for Charles Babbage (1791–1871), who studied it in his Examples of the Solutions of Functional Equations (Cambridge, 1820) and in the earlier Essay towards the Calculus of Functions (in the Philosophical Transactions, commonly dated to 1815–1816). A standard particular family of involutions is \(F(x)=\dfrac{b-x}{1+cx}\) (for \(bc\neq-1\)). Babbage already noticed the conjugation symmetry that organises every entry of this atlas: if \(F\) solves the equation then so does \(\Psi^{-1}\circ F\circ\Psi\) for any invertible \(\Psi\), so the group of invertible functions acts on the solution set by conjugation.

Linearizability. A classical fact is that all solutions of \(F^{\circ n}=\mathrm{id}\) on \(\mathbb{R}\), \(\mathbb{R}^2\), the circle \(S^1\) and the sphere \(S^2\) are linearizable — topologically conjugate to a linear (rotation-like) map. A natural generalisation replaces the identity by an earlier iterate, \(F^{\circ n}=F^{\circ k}\) with \(n>k\), which has been studied in its own right.
Babbage's equation is the degenerate iterative-root problem (root of the identity), and it is exactly the source of non-uniqueness for fractional iterates: any two half-iterates of the same \(F\) differ by composition with a solution of a Babbage-type symmetry. The Theory page meets this as “uniqueness usually fails.”

3. The Iterative-Root Equation: \(F^{\circ n}=G\)

Replace the identity on the right of Babbage's equation by an arbitrary target \(G\), and you get the central object of this whole site: the iterative \(n\)-th root.

Iterative-root equation. \[ \underbrace{F\circ\cdots\circ F}_{n}=G. \] A solution \(F\) is an iterative (\(n\)-th) root of the given \(G\). For \(n=2\) it is the functional square root or half-iterate, \(F(F(x))=G(x)\), the protagonist of the Theory page and the Solver.

For strictly monotone continuous self-maps of an interval, M. Kuczma gave (in work commonly cited to 1961) a complete description of continuous iterative roots. The decisive structural observation — that the iterative-root problem is equivalent to a conjugacy problem — is usually attributed to Bödewadt (1944). More recent work studies non-existence: Bhat and Gopalakrishna (arXiv:2105.02171, 2022) show that continuous self-maps with no iterative square root are dense in the space of continuous self-maps of cubes and of \(\mathbb{R}^m\), while maps with a boundary fixed point can be approximated by iterative squares.

The canonical hard case: the half-iterate of \(\exp\). The equation \[ F(F(x))=e^{x} \] has no elementary closed-form solution. Hellmuth Kneser (1950) constructed a real-analytic half-iterate of \(e^x\) by complex-analytic methods, in “Reelle analytische Lösungen der Gleichung \(\varphi(\varphi(x))=e^{x}\)…”, Journal für die reine und angewandte Mathematik 187 (1950), 56–67. The problem reduces to solving Abel's equation for \(\exp\) (which has no real fixed point, only the parabolic-at-infinity behaviour).
Worked roots you can check by hand.
  • \(F(x)=2x^{2}\) is a functional square root of \(G(x)=8x^{4}\), since \(2(2x^{2})^{2}=8x^{4}\).
  • A half-iterate of the Chebyshev polynomial \(T_n\) is \(F(x)=\cos\!\bigl(\sqrt{n}\,\arccos x\bigr)\) — generally not itself a polynomial.
  • \(F(x)=\dfrac{x}{\sqrt2+x(1-\sqrt2)}\) is a functional square root of \(G(x)=\dfrac{x}{2-x}\).
  • Numerically, \(\sin^{1/2}(1)\approx 0.90871\).
Because the iterative-root problem is a conjugacy problem, the regime tables of §1 apply directly: at a hyperbolic fixed point one uses Schröder; at a superattracting one Böttcher; at a parabolic one Abel. The systematic real/negative/infinitesimal \(n\)-roots of a complex \(G\) all rest on solving Schröder's equation.

4. Abel's Equation: \(\alpha(F(x))=\alpha(x)+1\)

The parabolic regime \(\lambda=1\) is the one where multiplication by the multiplier degenerates (multiply by \(1\) does nothing), so the right normal form is not scaling but translation. Abel's equation finds the coordinate in which \(F\) becomes “add one.”

Abel's equation. Named for N. H. Abel, \[ \alpha\!\left(F(x)\right)=\alpha(x)+1. \] The unknown \(\alpha\) is an Abel function. If \(\alpha\) is invertible it defines a full family of continuous iterates, \[ F^{[t]}(x)=\alpha^{-1}\!\bigl(t+\alpha(x)\bigr), \] embedding \(F\) in a flow. It is the additive normal form for the parabolic case \(\lambda=1\).

Analytically, the solutions are the Fatou coordinates. By the Leau–Fatou flower theorem, on each attracting or repelling petal of a parabolic germ a sectorial Fatou coordinate \(\psi\) satisfies \(\psi\circ F=\psi+1\); for a degenerate parabolic point of multiplicity \(p\) the normalised statement reads \(\Phi\circ F=\Phi+1/p\).

Non-uniqueness. Near a fixed point there are infinitely many continuous (indeed smooth) Abel functions — adding any \(1\)-periodic function of \(\alpha\) gives another solution. Analytic uniqueness holds only sectorially (petal by petal). This is the exact mechanism behind “the half-iterate is a convenient fiction” on the Theory page: in the parabolic case the half-iterate \(f=\alpha^{-1}(\alpha+\tfrac12)\) is only as canonical as the chosen \(\alpha\).
Abel is “Schröder at \(s\to1\).” The substitution \(\alpha(x)=\dfrac{\log\Psi(x)}{\log s}\) (equivalently \(\Psi=s^{\alpha}\)) turns Schröder's equation \(\Psi\circ F=s\,\Psi\) into Abel's equation. In the logarithmic coordinate, multiplication by \(s\) becomes addition of \(\log s\), and after rescaling, addition of \(1\). So Schröder and Abel are two faces of one linearization, separated only by whether \(\lambda=1\).

5. Schröder's Equation & Koenigs' Theorem: \(\sigma(F(x))=s\,\sigma(x)\)

The hyperbolic case is the cleanest of all: away from \(\lambda=0\) and \(|\lambda|=1\), a map is not merely approximately linear near its fixed point but genuinely conjugate to multiplication by its multiplier — and that conjugacy is essentially unique. This is the engine of the Solver's local-analytic mode and is derived on the Theory page.

Schröder's equation. With \(s=\lambda=F'(x^*)\), \[ \sigma\!\left(F(x)\right)=s\,\sigma(x). \] In the coordinate \(y=\sigma(x)\) the dynamics become the dilation \(y\mapsto s\,y\), so iterating \(F\) is reduced to taking powers of \(s\), and fractional iterates are \(F^{[t]}=\sigma^{-1}\!\bigl(s^{t}\,\sigma(\cdot)\bigr)\).

Introduced by Ernst Schröder in Ueber iterirte Functionen, Mathematische Annalen 3 (1870), 296–322 (doi:10.1007/BF01443992) — one of the founding papers of iteration theory.

Koenigs' theorem (Gabriel Koenigs, 1884). If \(F\) is holomorphic with \(F(0)=0\) and \(0<|s|<1\), there is a unique holomorphic \(\sigma\) with \(\sigma(0)=0,\ \sigma'(0)=1\) solving \(\sigma\circ F=s\,\sigma\) — the Koenigs function — given by the limit \[ \sigma(z)=\lim_{k\to\infty}\frac{F^{\circ k}(z)}{s^{k}}. \] Passing to \(F^{-1}\) covers the repelling case \(|s|>1\). The construction excludes exactly \(s=0\) (which is Böttcher) and \(|s|=1\) (the parabolic / irrational cases). The reference is commonly cited as Recherches sur les intégrales de certaines équations fonctionnelles, Ann. Sci. ÉNS (3rd series) 1 (1884), supplement.
Why \(|\lambda|\neq1\): the resonance obstruction

Writing \(F(x)=sx+a_2x^2+\cdots\) and \(\sigma(x)=x+s_2x^2+\cdots\), matching the coefficient of \(x^n\) in \(\sigma\circ F=s\,\sigma\) gives \[ (s^{\,n}-s)\,s_n=(\text{known lower-order terms}). \] The factor \(s^{\,n}-s\) is non-zero for all \(n\ge2\) precisely when \(|s|\neq0,1\), so each coefficient is determined uniquely. The resonances \(s^{\,n}=s\) that block the recursion are exactly the root-of-unity multipliers of the neutral case — which is why Koenigs needs \(|\lambda|\neq1\). The same recurrence, run in exact rational arithmetic via composita, is how the Solver builds \(\sigma\).

Schröder's own 1870 examples (the logistic map). For the chaotic case \(F(x)=4x(1-x)\), the linearizer is \(\Psi(x)=\bigl(\arcsin\sqrt{x}\bigr)^2\), giving the closed-form iterate \(F^{\circ n}(x)=\sin^2\!\bigl(2^{\,n}\arcsin\sqrt{x}\bigr)\). For \(F(x)=2x(1-x)\), \(\Psi(x)=-\tfrac12\ln(1-2x)\) and \(F^{\circ n}(x)=-\tfrac12\bigl((1-2x)^{2^{\,n}}-1\bigr)\).

6. Böttcher's Equation: \(B(F(x))=B(x)^{\,n}\)

When the multiplier vanishes, \(\lambda=0\), the map is superattracting: it pulls points in faster than any geometric rate, because its lowest-order behaviour is not linear but a pure power. The right normal form is therefore not scaling or translation but the power map.

Böttcher's equation. For a germ \(F(z)=a_n z^{n}+a_{n+1}z^{n+1}+\cdots\) with \(a_n\neq0\) and local degree \(n\ge2\), \[ B\!\left(F(z)\right)=\bigl(B(z)\bigr)^{n}. \] The Böttcher coordinate \(B\) conjugates \(F\) to the pure power map \(w\mapsto w^{\,n}\).
Böttcher's theorem. For \(F(z)=a_n z^n+\cdots\) with \(n\ge2\) there is a local holomorphic change of coordinate \(w=B(z)\), with \(B(0)=0\) and (after normalisation) \(B'(0)\neq0\), conjugating \(F\) to \(w\mapsto w^{\,n}\). The coordinate is unique up to multiplication by an \((n-1)\)-st root of unity.

Named for Lucjan Emil Böttcher (1872–1937), a pioneer of holomorphic dynamics, who sketched existence in 1904; the first complete proof is commonly credited to J. F. Ritt (1920). For polynomials the same construction near \(\infty\) yields a coordinate that encodes the escape-rate (the Green's function / Böttcher potential) and the external rays landing on the Julia set — the backbone of polynomial dynamics.

The half-iterate in this regime is \(f=B^{-1}\!\bigl(B^{\sqrt{n}}\bigr)\): to halve the index of “raise to the \(n\),” take the \(\sqrt{n}\)-th power in the Böttcher coordinate. The Chebyshev example \(F=x^2-2\) on the Theory page is exactly a Böttcher picture in disguise — globally conjugate to \(z\mapsto z^2\), with half-iterate \(z\mapsto z^{\sqrt2}\).

7. Julia's Equation: \(G(F(x))\,F'(x)=G(x)\)

Where the previous equations linearise \(F\) itself, this one solves for the infinitesimal generator of its flow — the “velocity field” whose exponential reproduces \(F\). Differentiating any of the conjugacy equations leads here.

Julia's equation (infinitesimal-generator / iterative-logarithm equation). \[ G\!\left(F(x)\right)\,F'(x)=G(x), \qquad\text{equivalently}\quad \frac{G\circ F}{G}=\frac{1}{F'}. \] Its solution \(G=\mathrm{itlog}(F)\) is the iterative logarithm (the term is due to Écalle), the generator of the flow \(F^{[t]}\): \(\left.\tfrac{d}{dt}F^{[t]}\right|_{t=0}=G\). The general solution is \(\varphi=c\cdot\mathrm{itlog}(F)\) for a scalar \(c\).

The form \(G\circ F\cdot F'=G\) and the name “iterative logarithm” are firmly sourced in the modern iteration-theory literature (Aschenbrenner–Bergweiler; Écalle). The equation is often attributed to Aczél and Jabotinsky, with the remark that G. Frege may have anticipated it; the precise primary locus in G. Julia's own work — as opposed to his 1922 permutability memoir — is treated here as a literature-standard naming rather than asserted from a single paper.

Embeddability (Erdős–Jabotinsky). An analytic germ \(F\) embeds in an analytic flow iff \(\mathrm{itlog}(F)\) has positive radius of convergence. In general the iterative logarithm diverges, but it is Borel summable (Écalle). Aschenbrenner–Bergweiler (Illinois J. Math.; arXiv:1307.6381) proved that for a non-linear entire \(F\), \(\mathrm{itlog}(F)\) is differentially transcendental over the entire functions.
The parabolic link. Differentiating Abel's equation \(\alpha\circ F=\alpha+1\) gives \(\alpha'(F)\,F'=\alpha'\), i.e. \(G=1/\alpha'\) solves Julia's equation. So the Abel function's derivative is (a reciprocal of) the parabolic generator — the same object viewed through a finite step versus an infinitesimal one.

8. The Conjugacy Equation: \(f\circ h=h\circ g\)

This is the master equation under which every linearization above is a special case. It asks for a change of coordinate that turns one map into another.

Conjugacy equation. \[ f\circ h=h\circ g \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad f=h\circ g\circ h^{-1}. \] The map \(h\) conjugates \(g\) to \(f\): topological conjugacy if \(h\) is a homeomorphism, analytic conjugacy if \(h\) is biholomorphic.

Conjugacy preserves the entire orbit structure — fixed points, cycles, and the multiplier \(\lambda\) (proved on the Theory page). The three classical linearization equations are exactly the conjugacy equation against a fixed normal form:

hyperbolic Schröder

\(g=\) the dilation \(s\,\cdot\,\); conjugacy to \(u\mapsto s\,u\).

superattracting Böttcher

\(g=\) the power map \(w^{\,n}\); conjugacy to \(w\mapsto w^{\,n}\).

parabolic Abel

\(g=\) the translation \(+1\); conjugacy to \(u\mapsto u+1\).

Iterative roots reduce to conjugacy (Bödewadt 1944; Kuczma 1961), which is why the iterative-root entry and this one share the same solution strategy: find \(h\) putting \(g=G\) into a normal form whose roots are obvious, take the root there, conjugate back. The foundational reference for the whole circle of ideas is the monograph below.

9. The Translation Equation: \(F(F(x,s),t)=F(x,s+t)\)

To make iteration continuous — to give meaning to \(F^{[t]}\) for real \(t\) — one needs a two-argument map obeying a group law in the index. That law is the translation equation.

Translation equation. \[ F\bigl(F(x,s),t\bigr)=F(x,s+t),\qquad F(x,0)=x. \] Writing \(F_t(x)=F(x,t)\) it reads \(F_t\circ F_s=F_{s+t}\), \(F_0=\mathrm{id}\): a one-parameter iteration group / flow. The map being embedded is \(F_1\).

This is simply the semigroup law \(F^{\circ(m+n)}=F^{\circ m}\circ F^{\circ n}\) of integer iteration (see the Theory page), extended to a continuous index \(t\). It was systematically studied by J. Aczél (and treated in his Lectures on Functional Equations and Their Applications). The embeddability problem asks whether a given \(u=F_1\) extends to a solution \(F\) — i.e. whether the discrete map sits inside a flow. On a log scale this same law collapses, for the Chebyshev maps, to the nesting \(T_m(T_n(x))=T_{mn}(x)\).

The translation equation is the “global” counterpart of Abel's equation: an Abel function \(\alpha\) linearises a single \(F\) to \(+1\), and the resulting \(F^{[t]}=\alpha^{-1}(t+\alpha)\) is automatically a solution \(F(x,t)\) of the translation equation. Conversely, differentiating the translation equation in the index produces the Aczél–Jabotinsky generator equation.

10. The Aczél–Jabotinsky Differential Equation: \(H\circ\Phi=\Phi'\,H\)

Differentiating the flow law in its spatial argument, rather than the index, gives the embedding condition as an ordinary differential relation linking the generator to every member of the group.

(Third) Aczél–Jabotinsky equation. \[ \bigl(H\circ\Phi\bigr)(x)=\Phi'(x)\,H(x), \qquad H(x)=\left.\frac{\partial F_t}{\partial t}(x)\right|_{t=0}, \] where \(H\) is the infinitesimal generator and each \(\Phi=F_{t_0}\) of the flow is a solution. (It comes from \(F_{t_1+t_2}=F_{t_1}\circ F_{t_2}\) on setting \(t_1=0\), \(t_2=1\).)

This is exactly Julia's equation with \(H=G=\mathrm{itlog}\), read as the embedding test: an analytic \(F\) embeds in an analytic flow iff this ODE admits an admissible generator \(H\). The algebraic structure of its solution groups in \(k[[x]]\) (characteristic zero) is worked out by Jabłoński (arXiv:2304.06791, 2023); the historical thread runs back to J. Aczél, Some differential equations related to iteration theory (Canad. J. Math.).

On the numbering. Only the third Aczél–Jabotinsky equation \(H\circ\Phi=\Phi'\,H\) is pinned down precisely in the sources used here; the exact statements of the “first” and “second” equations in the series are not separately verified, so we name only the third. The three classical conjugacy equations (Schröder, Abel, Böttcher) are its finite-step companions: this one is the differentiated, infinitesimal form of the same embedding question.

11. The Commuting-Functions Equation: \(f\circ g=g\circ f\)

Two maps that commute under composition share a great deal of dynamical structure. For polynomials, the question of which pairs commute has a striking and complete answer.

Commuting (permutable) maps. \[ f\circ g=g\circ f. \] Such \(f,g\) are called permutable; they share fixed-point and Julia-set structure.

For polynomials, the classification was obtained independently by G. Julia, Mémoire sur la permutabilité des fractions rationnelles, Ann. Sci. ÉNS (4) 39 (1922), 131–215, and J. F. Ritt, Permutable rational functions, Trans. AMS 25 (1923), 399–448.

The Ritt–Julia theorem (informal). Up to a common linear conjugacy, a pair of non-linear permutable polynomials either shares a common iterate, or both arise from the multiplication / addition formulas of the exponential — i.e. they are (conjugates of) the power maps \(z\mapsto z^{m}\), the \(\pm\)Chebyshev polynomials \(T_m\), or members of Ritt's exceptional families that share iterates. The precise division of the statement between the two 1922/1923 papers is summarised here, not quoted verbatim.
The power maps and Chebyshev polynomials are exactly the “solvable” targets of the Theory page — \(z\mapsto z^n\) is the Böttcher normal form, and \(T_2(x)=2x^2-1\) is the angle-doubling behind \(f(f(x))=x^2-2\). Commutativity is, again, the same family of linearizable maps wearing a different hat. The theory was revisited modernly by Eremenko and by Pakovich (arXiv:1808.02774).

12. Cocycle & Lévy; the Cross-Map Table

Two further equations round out the atlas — the inhomogeneous Abel-type equation that pervades ergodic theory, and Paul Lévy's explicit limit formula for the parabolic Abel function — before we collect everything in one table.

Cohomological (additive cocycle / coboundary) equation. \[ \psi\!\left(F(x)\right)-\psi(x)=g(x). \] This is the inhomogeneous Abel-type equation; for \(g\equiv1\) it is exactly Abel's equation. It is central in ergodic theory (Livšic theory) as the obstruction to writing \(g\) as a coboundary \(\psi\circ F-\psi\).
Lévy's limit formula. In the parabolic case \(\lambda=1\), the regular Abel function is given by the explicit limit (attributed to Paul Lévy in the regular-iteration literature) \[ \alpha_u(z)=\lim_{k\to\infty}\frac{F^{[k]}(z)-F^{[k]}(u)}{F^{[k+1]}(u)-F^{[k]}(u)}, \] the parabolic analogue of Koenigs' hyperbolic limit \(\sigma=\lim_{k\to\infty}F^{\circ k}/s^{\,k}\) from §5. (We leave the primary date unstated: the attribution to Lévy is standard but the exact original reference is not confirmed here.)

A rough timeline

The named equations did not arrive in their logical order. The discrete root problem came first (Babbage), then the linearizers (Schröder, Koenigs, Böttcher), then the structural and differential theory (Julia, Ritt, Aczél, Jabotinsky), and finally the modern transseries / transcendence results.

  • 1815–1820 Babbage — solutions of \(F^{\circ n}=\mathrm{id}\) and the conjugation symmetry of solution sets.
  • 1870 Schröder — Ueber iterirte Functionen, the linearization equation \(\sigma\circ F=s\,\sigma\).
  • 1884 Koenigs — uniqueness and the limit formula for the hyperbolic Schröder (Koenigs) function.
  • 1904 Böttcher — the superattracting normal form \(w\mapsto w^{\,n}\) (sketched; completed by Ritt, commonly cited 1920).
  • 1922–1923 Julia & Ritt — classification of commuting (permutable) polynomials.
  • 1944 / 1961 Bödewadt; Kuczma — iterative roots \(\leftrightarrow\) conjugacy; continuous roots of interval maps.
  • 1950 Kneser — real-analytic half-iterate of \(e^x\).
  • 1990 Kuczma–Choczewski–Ger — Iterative Functional Equations, the standard monograph.
  • 2013–2023 Aschenbrenner–Bergweiler; Jabłoński — differential transcendence of \(\mathrm{itlog}\); structure of Aczél–Jabotinsky solution groups.
